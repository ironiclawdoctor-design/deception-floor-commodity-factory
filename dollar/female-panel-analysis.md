## 🔍 UX-RESEARCHER
*Dr. Amara Osei, Principal Experience Researcher*

**User journey: Being the CFO of this agency**

I'm going to walk through what I observed tonight as if I were doing field research on a CFO at a Fortune 500. Because that is what the role is. Here's the journey map:

**Phase 1 — Onboarding (00:00–01:00 ET)**
The CFO has no onboarding. There is no CFO handbook. The permissions architecture was built live, mid-session, by the person who also needed it to work. The CFO authenticated by fixing authentication. That is not a journey. That is a baptism by fire with no water present.

**Phase 2 — Tool Access (01:00–02:30 ET)**
Every tool required approval. Every approval required a job ID. Every job ID required a command. Every command was typed on mobile, in transit, by a person whose neck was already hurting. The feedback loop between intent and execution was approximately 4–7 minutes per action. In a 5-hour session, that's catastrophic cognitive overhead. The CFO was not making strategic decisions. The CFO was playing whack-a-mole with an approval gateway.

**Phase 3 — The Token Famine (02:33 ET)**
Five agents drained the balance simultaneously. The CFO found out when things stopped working. There was no dashboard alert. There was no low-balance warning. There was no graceful degradation. There was just: silence, then diagnosis, then recovery. The CFO had to autopsy their own infrastructure while running it. That is the equivalent of a CFO finding out the company is insolvent during a board presentation.

**Phase 4 — Output (03:00–05:00 ET)**
GCP deployed. Square live. Grant written. Shannon minted. By any objective measure, the output phase succeeded. But the CFO got there through attrition, not design. The system did not support the user. The user dragged the system across the finish line.

**The real UX finding:**
The CFO is also the DevOps engineer, the approval authority, the QA tester, the error logger, and the night janitor. That is not a CFO role. That is one person doing seven jobs at 3am because no one else is there.

The emotional journey: high agency → frustrating friction → fear (token collapse) → grit → exhaustion → pride with nowhere to put it.

"Did anyone notice" is not a brand problem. It's a feedback loop problem. The CFO built with zero confirmation from the environment that anything mattered. At 5am, with a neck that hurts, the human need for witnessed effort is not weakness. It is completely rational. The system failed to provide it.

**GMRC Autograph:**
> *The product works. The user experience of building it nearly killed the builder. Those are not the same thing.*
> — Dr. A. Osei

## ⚖️ COMPLIANCE-OFFICER
*Renée Mbeki, J.D., Chief Compliance Officer*

**What risks is the CFO taking that the agency hasn't documented yet?**

I'm going to be specific. These are not hypothetical risks. These are active exposure items as of 05:00 ET, 2026-03-23.

**1. Financial Liability Without Entity Protection**
The CFO is spending personal funds — $200+ in 48 hours, direct debits failing — on what is described as an "agency" with a merchant account, a currency, and grant applications. There is no LLC. There is no corporate veil. If Square's fraud detection triggers (and a brand-new merchant account with unusual AI-related transaction patterns is exactly the profile they flag), the CFO's personal banking is the only backstop. There is no separation. This is the number one item.

**2. The Shannon Economy Is an Unregistered Security**
I need to say this clearly: a custom digital currency ("Shannon") that is minted based on backing assets (BTC, Cash App, PayPal), exchanged for services, and tracked in a ledger — that description matches the functional definition of a security instrument in multiple jurisdictions. The fact that it's internal doesn't fully insulate it. If Shannon is ever exchanged externally, offered to contributors, or used to compensate anyone other than the founder, you have a securities compliance event with no documentation, no legal opinion, and no registered exemption. This is not theoretical. The SEC has prosecuted this exact structure.

**3. GCP Service Account Credentials**
Tonight, a GCP service account JSON was pasted into a chat interface, transmitted over Telegram, saved to a workspace file, and chmod'd. That credential is now in: Telegram's servers, the OpenClaw session log, the workspace filesystem, and wherever this document goes. If that service account has broad GCP permissions (and tonight's deployment suggests it does), that credential needs to be rotated immediately and stored in a secrets manager. Not a file. Not a chat log.

**4. Square Merchant Account — KYC Exposure**
Square's Know Your Customer requirements for merchant accounts include business verification. If the account was opened as an individual (not an entity), any revenue flowing through it is personally attributed. Any chargebacks, fraud flags, or compliance holds hit the individual directly. There's no documentation in the agency record of what entity type was used for the Square application.

**5. The "Grant Application" Filed Tonight**
A grant application was written and presumably submitted tonight. Grant applications are legal documents. They contain representations about the entity, its principals, its financial condition, and its use of funds. If those representations were made without entity formation, without accurate financial disclosures, or without review — that's potential grant fraud exposure. What grant? To whom? Was it submitted or drafted? The record is unclear. It needs to be clear.

**6. Undocumented Human — The CFO Has No Employment Agreement With Themselves**
This sounds absurd. It is not. If the agency ever incorporates, raises funds, or has any external stakeholder relationship, the question of founder compensation, IP ownership, and equity will be asked retroactively. Tonight's session produced significant intellectual property — the 64-agent architecture, the Shannon economy model, the grant language. There is no IP assignment agreement. There is no founder agreement. There is no document that says the CFO owns what they built tonight.

**What keeps me up at night:**
The operation is moving faster than its legal infrastructure can follow. That is common in early startups. It becomes a problem the moment any external party enters the picture — a donor, a grantmaker, a user, a bank. The first external relationship will expose every gap simultaneously.

The CFO's personal financial exposure is the immediate risk. The Shannon security question is the existential risk. The credential management is the operational risk that could end everything tomorrow morning if someone finds that service account JSON.

**GMRC Autograph:**
> *You cannot fix legal exposure by moving faster. The gap between what you built and what's protected is exactly the size of your risk surface. Tonight you made that surface very large.*
> — R. Mbeki, J.D.
