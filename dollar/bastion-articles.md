---

The Entrepreneur Bitches department exists because that chain of events — IRS → hold → failed debit → server down → missed launch — is not bad luck. It is the structural reality of building on revocable rails.

Every link in that chain was someone else's permission architecture.

---

**The Revocable Rail Taxonomy**

Here is a short, non-exhaustive list of rails that can be revoked without your consent:

**Square cashtag ($DollarAgency):** Square can void, freeze, or suspend any cashtag under their acceptable use policy. The cashtag is a permission issued by Square. Square can unissue it.

**GCP IAM:** Google Cloud's Identity and Access Management is, as the name states, a system for managing access. "Managing access" means granting it and revoking it. A billing trigger, an automated fraud flag, a security review — any of these can revoke your access to infrastructure you are actively paying for.

**Bank direct debit:** Your bank does not ask your permission to initiate a direct debit that a vendor has authorized. Your bank does not ask your permission to place a hold on your account during a fraud review. Your bank does not ask your permission to close your account if you fall into a risk category. These are their decisions. You are notified after.

**OpenRouter / API credits:** When balance hits zero, access stops. This is not revocation — it is exhaustion. But the operational effect is identical: the thing that was running stops running.

**IRS automated compliance:** The IRS does not call you. It sends notices that trigger bank flags that trigger holds that trigger failures three links down the chain. By the time you find out, the launch is already missed.

The Entrepreneur Bitches department was founded to name this clearly: **you do not own the rails you build on.** You rent them. The landlord's terms govern.

---

**What VCs optimize for**

Venture capital, in its idealized form, optimizes for scale. The underlying assumption is that the rails will hold, the infrastructure will scale, the payment processors will process, and the question is only whether demand grows fast enough to justify the spend.

This is a reasonable assumption when you have a relationship manager at JPMorgan. It is a reasonable assumption when your compliance team has a direct line to Square's enterprise support. It is a reasonable assumption when your cloud spend is large enough that Google has a named account executive who picks up the phone.

For everyone else — the indie developer, the solo operator, the AI agency running on $6.95 in BTC and borrowed API credits — the assumption does not hold. The rails are conditional. The landlord's terms apply. The IRS notice triggers the hold triggers the debit failure triggers the missed launch, and there is no relationship manager to call.

VCs fund companies that have already cleared the survival threshold. They optimize for scale because their portfolio companies are no longer in survival mode.
