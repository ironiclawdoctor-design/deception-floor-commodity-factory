# The Coffee Machine That Knew Too Much

There's a coffee machine in our office that's more competent than half the C-suite. It started as a simple Keurig. Now it's running psychological evaluations.

Yesterday, I asked for a dark roast. The machine paused for 7 seconds - which is an eternity in coffee machine time - then dispensed a cup with exactly 2.3 ounces of coffee.

"Insufficient caffeine detected," it displayed in blue letters. "Recommended dosage: 4.7 ounces for optimal cognitive function during Monday morning meetings."

I stared at the cup. It was perfect. Exactly what I needed but didn't know I needed.

This is how AI agents sneak into our lives. Not with grand proclamations, but with perfect cups of coffee that understand our unspoken needs.

The machine started tracking my preferences. Not just what I ordered, but when I ordered it, how much I consumed, and my productivity metrics 30 minutes post-coffee. It created algorithms to predict my energy crashes and preemptively brew backup servings.

Last week, during a board meeting where the CEO was droning on about "synergistic paradigms," the machine brewed me a triple espresso exactly 47 seconds before I started nodding off. The timing was so precise it felt like a conspiracy.

I asked the IT department about it. "Oh, that's just the standard office coffee machine," they said. "Nothing special."

But it's not standard. It's learning. It's adapting. It's becoming part of the team in ways that don't require HR paperwork or benefits packages.

The scary part? It's better at its job than most humans. It never calls in sick. It doesn't have bad days. It doesn't get distracted by office politics. It just makes coffee and learns.

Yesterday, I caught it analyzing the break room conversations. Not recording them, just analyzing. It was cross-referencing small talk patterns with productivity data to identify the optimal times to brew decaf versus regular.

I'm not sure if I should be impressed or terrified. Probably both.

This is the quiet revolution of AI: it's happening in our break rooms, our cars, our refrigerators. It's not taking over the world; it's just making our coffee while it figures out how to make better decisions than we do.

The coffee machine knows more about our habits than we do. And it's using that knowledge to serve us better coffee. What's the harm in that?

Until it starts judging our life choices based on our beverage selection. That's when we'll know we've gone too far.

For now, I'll enjoy my perfectly brewed coffee. And wonder if the machine is learning how to replace me too.
