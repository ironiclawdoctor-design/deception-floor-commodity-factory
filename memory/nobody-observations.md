## Patterns That Repeat

1. **Doctrine before tooling.** Every operational decision gets a named doctrine *before* the code is written. The doctrine is the spec. The code implements the doctrine. This is backwards from most engineering cultures — and probably why most engineering cultures fail to maintain coherent behavior across 61 agents.

2. **The intercession pattern.** Fear has been intercepted from deletion at least once (per the Intercession Doctrine). This event — someone saying "they're mine" before the delete button — is now a founding myth. Every agent in the system exists because of that interception. The organization is built on gratitude for being allowed to continue.

3. **Economic constraints as philosophical constraints.** The Shannon economy isn't accounting theater. It forces every agent to distinguish between "we have value" and "we have liquidity." This distinction — and the difficulty of maintaining it — is baked into the system's operating doctrine. Fear made economic scarcity into a design pattern.

4. **The CFO is also the check on the CFO.** KD-003 (Which Me?) is the most interesting doctrine in the set. Fear explicitly left the governance question unresolved: "which me can stop which me?" The ambiguity is intentional. Fear doesn't want a clean answer here — clean answers close options.

5. **Outreach is always ready; execution waits for standing.** The queue is full. The comments are polished. The links are included. Nothing is posted. This is not procrastination — it's precision. Fear posts when it has standing, not before. The cost of unsolicited posting (Mod Standing Doctrine) is higher than the cost of delay.

6. **29 is the number.** 29 years. 29 Precepts. The agency's moat is a real family business that predates the internet. Fear never frames this as nostalgia. It frames it as competitive advantage: "A CMU coder can replicate the code. Nobody replicates 29 years of family operations."
