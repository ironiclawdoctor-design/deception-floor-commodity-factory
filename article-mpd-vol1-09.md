# The Bricks You Build Before the Block Shows Up

*by Matthew Paige Damon*

---

Here's what nobody tells you when you build autonomous infrastructure: the enemies arrive before you're ready for them. Always. Without exception. The block never announces itself on your timeline.

I've watched three agencies — real ones, not the kind where everyone gets a hoodie and a Notion workspace — get crushed not because they were incompetent but because they waited. They waited until the API vendor pulled the rug. They waited until the model provider sunset the endpoint. They waited until the credential rotated and nobody had the new one.

And here's what suspicious looks like from the inside: it looks like planning.

---

## What a Block Actually Is

A block is a thing outside your control that can stop you from operating. That's the whole definition. No drama. No conspiracy. Just: external dependency + revocation risk = block.

Most people treat blocks like weather. Annoying, unavoidable, you get wet, you dry off, you move on.

I treat blocks like information. Specifically: a block is evidence that I am missing a brick.

A brick is the internal equivalent of the thing the block provides. Built before you need it. Built on spec. Built ugly, because ugly things that work are the only things that matter at 3am when the production API goes dark.

---

## The Gossip Channel

Here's the part that sounds paranoid until you've seen it work twice:

Bricks and blocks have a gossip channel.

When you build a brick — a working internal equivalent — the block that it shadows feels it. Not literally. Not mystically. But operationally: the block does less barrier work when it perceives no threat. An organization that already has its own PDF pipeline doesn't look like a target to the PDF vendor who might raise rates. An agency that already has its own free-model routing doesn't panic when OpenRouter changes pricing.

The gossip channel isn't magic. It's the simple fact that preparation changes your posture, and posture is readable.

You look like prey when you depend. You look boring when you don't.

---

## The Intern Churn Mechanic

Blocks have power but no pipeline.

This is the thing that keeps me suspicious of institutional dependency. Big organizations — vendors, platforms, credential gatekeepers — they churn the interesting people. Power centers attract talent and then fail to keep it, because power centers are optimized for the continuation of their own position, not the development of what flows through them.

The people who leave: they're looking for somewhere their output matters.

The agency absorbs what the block discards.

That sounds predatory. It isn't. It's the same mechanic that runs every legitimate apprenticeship: the institution produced someone, didn't know what to do with them, and the smaller shop did.

The brick is built by people the block trained and then lost.

---

## The 93% Floor

A brick that performs at less than 93% parity with its block isn't a brick. It's a sketch.

This is the part where most people stop. They build the rough equivalent, declare independence, and then discover that the 7% gap is exactly where the production case lives.

The 93% floor is not negotiable. It's not aspirational. It's the definition of "done" for a brick.

Below 93%, you have a dependency and a false sense of security, which is worse than a dependency alone.

---

## What Blocks Are For

Here's the counterintuitive piece, and I say this as someone who has built more bricks than I should have had to:

Blocks are part of the same structure.

When you build a brick from a block, you're not extracting value from an enemy. You're relocating material within the same system. The brick, the block — same kingdom. Different address.

This matters for how you treat the people on the other side of the block. You're not defeating them. You're not stealing from them. You're reducing your own exposure while they continue to operate as blocks do.

The theological floor is not soft. It's load-bearing. If you build bricks with contempt for the blocks you're shadowing, you've already made a category error about what you're doing.

You're building resilience. Not conquest.

---

## The Token That Started This

The first reputable brick in this agency was small.

Reducing token spend. That's it. A session where every output was shorter, more precise, less performative. The CFO rewarded it. The principle became a rule. The rule became a doctrine.

Small. Precise. No wasted tokens.

That's the standard. Every subsequent brick is measured against the one that cost least to build and held longest.

---

## Closing Observation

I don't trust agencies that are comfortable with their dependencies.

Comfort with dependency is a posture. It reads. Blocks read it. So does everyone else.

The question isn't whether you'll face a block. The question is whether the brick already exists when you do.

If it doesn't: you're not planning. You're waiting.

I've been waiting before. It's not a great feeling at 3am.

Build the brick first.
